# UNTITLED — Story Bible
*Working document. Updated as of first brainstorm session.*

---

## One Line Pitch
A hunter and their hunted are star-crossed lovers in a dynasty poised to redeem them, too late for their love.

---

## Paragraph Summary
A young hunter inherits a broken legacy from a mentor they know only as myth, unaware the mentor is a Tender who hunted their own kind out of ancient self-hatred. Tracking a Tender to its lair, the Young finds instead a stranger kneeling over a body — the Child, who killed the Tender deliberately to earn the Young's trust. The Young takes them on. A crisis binds them. The Child confesses. The Young buries it. Then something breaks the disguise, and the Young sees what the Child is. The fight that follows has no heart in it. They end up in each other's arms anyway, and the Young gives up the hunt, unable to reconcile what they were made to be with what they feel. The dynasty heals. The love does not.

---

## World

- Tenders enter human dreams. This is not predation — it is tending. Spirits and humans were once partners: Tenders entered dreams and lent power, humans channeled that power unconsciously to make things better. A collaboration that kept the world whole.
- The Old poisoned this. Taught humans that Tender visitation in dreams was violation and intrusion. Reframed caregiving as trespass.
- Humans closed themselves off. The magic stopped flowing. The kingdom's disarray is a symptom of this broken collaboration, though no one has named it yet.
- When people dream of animals, they believe it is an omen. It is Tenders. The fear is the Old's legacy.
- There has always been only one real hunter — the one with the weapon. Most other hunters are self-taught and self-proclaimed, far less effective.
- The weapon is not special. It is a symbol. The Old's power was their own, not the weapon's. Everyone — the queen, the Balahot, the Child — built their fear and their plans around a myth.

---

## The Beings

- Called **Tenders** by themselves. Called **Balahot** by humans — a word that became a curse over generations, synonymous with nightmare and misfortune.
- True forms are ancient, animal-adjacent, shapeshifting.
- Can disguise themselves as humans. An undertrained hunter cannot spot them.
- Aesthetic reference: kitsune energy — but original to this world. Not culturally Japanese.
- Rooted in Levantine mythology. Balahot derives from Hebrew.
- They do not kill each other without consequence. Killing a Tender is permanent and grave.

## Characters

### The Hunter
- The original hunter. The "vigilante" — in quotes deliberately.
- To Anat, a myth. A legend. The closest thing she had to a parent or a teacher.
- Unknown to Anat: the Hunter is a Tender. Ancient. Self-loathing.
- The Hunter's agenda: born different. Where other Tenders experience tending as joy and fulfillment, the Hunter experienced it as agony. Every dream they entered cost them something. They couldn't stop, or wouldn't. So they burned the whole system down and called it righteousness. Not a villain's agenda — a wound that became a worldview.
- The Hunter was coming to an end. Anat found them and was relentless — she followed, she stayed, she refused to leave. The Hunter let her. Eventually grew to like her. Never actually taught her much.
- Anat believes she was chosen and shaped. She was not. She was a stubborn child who wore down a dying creature that didn't mind the company.
- The Hunter is the source of the weapon — which is not special — and of whatever fragments of craft Anat carries.
- Anat spends the whole story trying to be worthy of someone who never told her the truth about anything.

### Anat
- Inherits the hunter role before fully completing their training.
- Cannot reliably spot Tenders in human form at the start of the story.
- Carries the Hunter's weapon — believed by all to be the source of power. It is not.
- First person narrator.
- Is not redeemed. The dynasty is.
- Never overcomes their ingrained hatred of Tenders. Loves this one Tender, but only in human form. Cannot love the true form. The prejudice does not break — only the ability to act on it.
- Gives up the hunt not out of growth or belief that Tenders deserve to live, but out of grief. Cannot kill what they love. That is not the same thing as redemption.
- Does not believe she is desirable. The Hunter never told her she was good enough, never told her she was chosen, never actually taught her anything. Anat filled that silence with relentless effort. The hunt is the only context in which she knows how to be valued.
- When Gil confesses, Anat's first instinct is disbelief. Not self-denial. Not noble sacrifice. Just the quiet certainty that Gil must be wrong. Nobody looks at her that way. She deflects to protect herself from hope, because hope feels more dangerous than rejection.
- The hunt and the deflection are the same wound expressed twice. She is chasing the Hunter's approval and simultaneously cannot believe anyone would choose her. Both are the Hunter's legacy — inherited from someone who never told her the truth about anything.

### Nofet Dvash / Gil
- A Tender in human form.
- True name: **Nofet Dvash** — an ancient Hebrew phrase meaning "flowing with honey." A Tender name, untranslatable, predating human naming conventions. The reader sees it once in the golden scenes.
- Human name: **Gil**. Plain, ungendered, chosen to disappear into.
- The same being as a Tender imprisoned for a century, captured at age seventeen.
- Released by the queen on crowning day. Walks out into freedom and is not ready for the mission. Spends time simply existing — feeling the sun, moving without walls. Her hope for herself slowly recovers. Her hope for her people remains cracked.
- Zahara is aging. The window is real. Gil feels the weight of the debt and the guilt of her own paralysis. She forces herself toward the mission eventually — not out of pure conviction but because the guilt becomes unbearable.
- The mission is built on a false premise. The weapon is not special. There is no weakness to find.
- Finds the older Tender — someone she loves — and asks them to sacrifice their life so Gil can use the body to earn Anat's trust. This costs her. The grief and guilt of it she carries into everything that follows.
- Does not trust humans. Is surprised when Zahara follows through on crowning day. Zahara is the first crack in that distrust.
- Anat is the second — but ambiguously. Anat loves Gil, but only in human form. Whether this proves Gil right or wrong about humans is left to the reader.
- Falls in love with Anat for real. The mission and the love become incompatible.
- The confession is the moment Gil chooses the person over the mission.

### Zahara
- Named **Zahara** — to bloom, to shine. Something becoming rather than already arrived.
- Became queen at literal birth. Advisors ran the kingdom through her childhood. She is slowly taking the reins.
- Not yet fully indoctrinated into the dynasty's hatred of Tenders — too young, too curious.
- As a child, sneaks away to visit the imprisoned Child. Returns repeatedly over years. A friendship forms through stone — possibly something deeper. The Child is the one constant relationship in her life with no political agenda.
- The Child watches her grow up. The queen grows up with her enemy and sees their humanity.
- They build a plan together over years of whispered conversations — not as a transaction, but as two people who trust each other dreaming out loud.
- On her official crowning day — the first moment she holds real power — she releases the Child. Her first act as queen. A promise kept.
- She is the dynasty's redemption arc. She is not seeking to restore magic or fix the world cosmologically. She is seeking justice for the Tenders. The larger consequences of the restored collaboration are beyond her current understanding.

## Plot (Chronological)

1. The Hunter dies. Anat inherits the role, undertrained, before fully understanding the craft.
2. Zahara releases the imprisoned Gil with a mission.
3. An older Tender deliberately manifests bad dreams in a nearby village, drawing Anat in.
4. Anat tracks the disturbance to a lair. Finds Gil kneeling over the older Tender's body. Gil killed them — deliberately — to earn Anat's trust.
5. Anat, seeing what Gil is capable of, takes her on as a protégé.
6. They return to the village haunted by misfortune. First crisis — human, not supernatural. The older Tender's bad dreams caused real damage: the ox died, the field is unplowed, someone is sick. Anat puts down her sword and helps pull the plow. Tends to the sick. Does the backbreaking unglamorous work without being asked. Gil watches. The mission starts cracking here.
7. They leave the village. Travel. The bond deepens on the road — nights around fires, days side by side.
8. The love has formed.
9. Gil confesses feelings. Anat deflects — hiding reciprocal feelings, holding back because of the perceived age gap, not knowing Gil is a Tender. Exact wording TBD.
10. Second crisis — bandits on the road. Anat steps in front of danger for Gil without thinking. — human. Anat sacrifices herself for Gil without hesitation.
11. Gil, far more powerful than she has appeared, heals Anat using her true power. Cannot pretend anymore.
12. Anat wakes. Sees the golden glow in Gil's eyes. The connection made.
13. A fight with no heart in it. They end up in each other's arms. Anat gives up the hunt but cannot reconcile her feelings. She is not redeemed.
14. The dynasty heals. Zahara succeeds. The world becomes something better. The love does not survive.

**Note:** The story is told out of chronological order for dramatic effect. The queen's arc — which is already underway before the Young knows — is revealed after.

## Structure & Format

- Approximately 34 pages, with room to expand to 40.
- First person narration from the Young's POV (caption boxes).
- **The Young's scenes: blue palette.**
- **The Queen's scenes: golden yellow palette.**
- Queen scenes are interspersed throughout, showing the Child in true form across multiple conversations.
- The queen scenes crescendo to the moment the Child shifts into human form and the reader sees it is the same face. The reader connects the dots. The Young may never know the full history.
- The golden color is also a foreshadowing system — the tell the reader has been looking at all along.

## Character Summaries

### Anat
- Named **Anat** — after the Canaanite goddess of war and hunting. Aspirational. Named by someone who believed she would become something.
- **Goal:** Earn the Hunter's pride posthumously. Prove the inheritance was deserved.
- **Motivation:** The hunt is the only context in which she knows how to be valued. She has no self outside the role, and no belief that anyone would choose her outside of it.
- **Conflict:** Falls in love with the thing she was made to destroy — and cannot believe the feeling is returned.
- **Change:** Learns Tenders are not monsters. Cannot unlearn the hate anyway. Stops hunting not from growth but from grief. The wound the Hunter left — you are not enough, you are not wanted — does not heal. It just loses its only justification.

### Nofet Dvash / Gil
- **Goal:** End the hunting. Free Tenders from persecution.
- **Motivation:** A century of imprisonment clarified her. She will not let what happened to her happen to others. But underneath the mission: she wants to simply exist in freedom, and cannot let herself.
- **Conflict:** Falls in love with the hunter she was sent to disarm.
- **Change:** Walks out of the cage hopeless about her people, paralyzed between her own freedom and her obligation. Forces herself forward out of guilt, not conviction. Learns to trust Zahara. Learns to love Anat. Does not learn to trust humanity broadly. Whether that is growth or just two exceptions is left open.

### Zahara
- **Goal:** Justice for Tenders.
- **Motivation:** She grew up with one. She knows what her dynasty built. She cannot unknow it.
- **Conflict:** She is trying to dismantle something her entire court believes is righteous, with the new and fragile power of a young crown.
- **Change:** Begins wanting to free this one Tender — the child she knows, the friend behind the stone. It is personal, not political. The Child teaches her that you cannot free one and leave the rest. She ends as someone who understands the wrong is systemic. She becomes a ruler rather than a girl keeping a promise.

## Open Questions

- What exactly draws the Young to the lair in the opening scene? The older Tender deliberately manifests bad dreams in a nearby village, causing an uptick of misfortune that draws the Young in. Tenders of old only manifested good dreams — the Old convinced the world otherwise.
- What do Tenders do that makes humans afraid of them? *(The Old reframed dream-tending as predation — but what specific fears did they seed?)*
- Levantine mythological research for grounding.
